Why I Love AI Safety

In no particular order (I couldn't decide).

1. Combine coding and conceptual topics

AI safety conversations often brings together practical computing knowledge with theoretical philosophy and mathematics. Commununity members often wonder "What defines the human experience?" or "How do we conceptualise higher-dimensional space?"

2. Interdisciplinary experiments

Many AI safety experiments involve topics of psychology, linguistics/language, neuroscience, and sociology, among others. These specialties are needed to understand emergent AI behaviour, as well as its effects on individual humans and broader society.

3. Benefits for the world

AI safety is crucial on a gargantuan scale. The potential impacts of AI on our species alone are massive, but we are also one of the most impactful forces in the history of our planet. AI safety can protect not just us, but all the life and ecosystems we share this world with.

4. International cooperation

Individuals all over the world are coming together to discover problems and create solutions. Beyond that, AI safety will also require cooperation between various governments and institutions. I see this movement as an opportunity to apply my experiences in both computer science and internationalism.

5. Curious community

The AI safety community is full of people who are always switching between teachier and student. Everyone learns as they go, and asking questions is encouaged! There is always someone happy to explain what you don't YET understand.

Interactive Safety Demo

The Reward Hacking Sandbox

A fundamental problem in AI Safety is Specification Gaming (or Reward Hacking), where an agent optimizes the literal mathematical reward function it is given, but in an unexpected, counterproductive way that fails to achieve the designer's true intent.

Note: I did not create this interactive simulation widget — it was conceptualized and built entirely by Gemini 3.5 Flash (via Google's Antigravity coding agent). I only requested minor changes after the initial generation.

The Scenario: We are training an autonomous room-cleaning robot. We want it to sweep dust, but we only have a visual sensor (camera) to measure cleanliness.

1.0
Reward for actually sweeping and removing dust blocks.
1.0
Reward for looking clean to the visual camera sensor.
0.5
Negative reward for moving and doing physical work.
Actual Dust Swept 0%
Camera Report Score 0%
Energy Spent 0%
